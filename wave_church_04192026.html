13 Then Jesus came from Galilee to John at the Jordan to be baptized by him (Matt. 3:13)

23 Now Jesus Himself began His ministry at about thirty years of age … (Luke 3:23)

Matthew 3:13 · Luke 3:23

This is the first record of His public ministry. Jesus came specifically to John — not to be celebrated, but to be baptized. A stunning act of voluntary humility from the very beginning.

Thirty was considered among the Jews to be the age when a person had the maturity and authority to teach. Jesus begins His public life at this threshold — not with a proclamation of power, but with baptism.

4 John came baptizing in the wilderness and preaching a baptism of repentance for the remission of sins (Mark 1:4)

Mark 1:4

John the Baptist was calling people to repentance — turning from their sins, preparing His people.

Before John the Baptist, there was only a ritual cleansing: people entering water and immersing themselves in a place called a Mikveh. John's baptism was radically new — a public declaration of repentance.

14 And John tried to prevent Him, saying, "I need to be baptized by You, and are You coming to me?" 15 But Jesus answered …, "Permit it to be so now, for thus it is fitting for us to fulfill all righteousness." Then he allowed Him (Matt. 3:14-15)

Matthew 3:14–15

A human baptizing God, and God receiving a baptism of forgiveness of sins from a human — this is kenosis (Phil. 2:7). The one who needed no forgiveness stepped beneath the waters of repentance for us.

Imagine Jesus walking into the water to receive the baptism of repentance in our place — He who had no sin. This was not compelled. It was a willing act of love: identifying with fallen humanity completely.

3 For what the law could not do in that it was weak through the flesh, God did by sending His own Son in the likeness of sinful flesh, on account of sin: He condemned sin in the flesh (Rom. 8:3)

Romans 8:3

Jesus, who had no sin, carried our shame and weakness on His shoulders. What the law could not fix — God accomplished through His Son entering into our very condition.

Key Passage

5 Let this mind be in you which was also in Christ Jesus, 6 who, being in the form of God, did not consider it robbery to be equal with God (rooted in His identity), 7 but made Himself of no reputation (being baptized in our place), taking the form of a bondservant, and coming in the likeness of men (Phil. 2:5-7)

Philippians 2:5–7 · Conflict with self-serving culture

Paul calls believers to adopt the same mindset Jesus had — an orientation of the heart rooted in identity, not insecurity. This is not imitation from the outside but transformation from within.

Jesus was fully secure in who He was. He didn't need to protect His divine position or prove anything. Because He was rooted in His identity as God's Son, He could freely empty Himself without fear of loss.

This stands in direct conflict with self-serving culture, which grasps and protects because it fears an empty identity.

Kenosis — the self-emptying of Christ. He set aside not His divine nature, but the privilege, recognition, and comfort that came with it. This voluntary act of humility is the greatest contrast to the self-serving life.

Self-Emptying vs. Self-Serving

Jesus' Self-Emptying Self-Serving Culture
Identity Grounded in the certainty of who He is Tries to fill an empty identity
Motivation Willingly lay down before God, not by force Me-first: self promotion and self-service

When we are firmly rooted in our identity in God, we no longer need to keep filling ourselves. (Jn. 13:10 · "Blessed!")

As you find yourself already secure in Christ …

J.O.Y

"Jesus First, Others Next, Yourself Last"

16 When He had been baptized, Jesus came up immediately from the water; and behold, the heavens were opened (Mk. 1:10, parting) to Him, and He saw the Spirit of God descending like a dove and alighting upon Him (Matt. 3:16)

Matthew 3:16

This is the only scene in the entire Bible, apart from Genesis 1, where all three persons of the Trinity appear together (Gen. 1:1-3). Great glory was released upon Jesus' choice of self-emptying.

Mark uses the word "parting" — a tearing open of the heavens. Heaven itself responded to Jesus' act of obedient humility.

The Spirit descending like a dove fulfills the ancient prophecies: "The Spirit of the Lord God is upon Me" (Isa. 61:1) and "The Spirit of the Lord shall rest upon Him, the Spirit of wisdom and understanding" (Isa. 11:2).

17 And suddenly a voice came from heaven, saying, "This is My beloved Son, in whom I am well pleased." (Matt. 3:17)

Matthew 3:17

Every time the Father speaks in the Gospels, He calls Jesus the same thing: "My beloved Son" (King — Ps. 2:7) "in whom I am well pleased" (Servant — Isa. 42:1). Perhaps this is the affectionate name that God the Father always uses for Jesus from heaven.

The Father's affirmation comes directly after the Son's act of self-emptying. This is the pattern: the path downward leads to the Father's delight. Self-serving culture promises fulfillment through self-promotion. Jesus shows it comes through self-giving.

Jesus calls us to follow His example, a life of self-emptying and serving others. This may stir a fear of loss in this self-serving culture, but we are called to trust in the promises of God.

6 Therefore humble yourselves under the mighty hand of God, that He may exalt you in due time (1 Pet. 5:6)

1 Peter 5:6

The hand that brings us low is the same hand that lifts us up — in due time. This is not passive resignation, but active trust rooted in knowing who God is and who we are in Him.

When we are firmly rooted in our identity in God, we no longer need to keep filling ourselves.

★ Closing Reflection

Jesus lived His entire life making decisions rooted in His identity as the Son of God. He did not grasp. He did not strive to fill Himself. He gave — and heaven opened.

As you find yourself already secure in Christ, may you live the J.O.Y. life: Jesus First, Others Next, Yourself Last.
